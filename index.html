Urban Resilience · Water · Pollution

Flooding-driven Pollution in Gowanus

As sea levels rise and storms intensify, the Gowanus Canal becomes a conduit for contaminated water entering the surrounding neighborhood — threatening public health, fragile ecology, and urban infrastructure.

Explore the research ↓

Health Risk

Toxic Exposure

Sewage overflow and industrial residues expose residents to bacteria, heavy metals, and PAHs during flood events.

Ecological Risk

Aquatic Collapse

Repeated contamination disrupts marine life recovery and degrades sediment quality across the canal basin.

Urban Risk

Infrastructure Strain

Aging combined sewer systems and low-lying streets amplify damage to homes, roads, and public space.

Section 02

How Pollution Forms

Two hydrological mechanisms drive contamination during heavy rain and tidal surges.

Overflow

During intense rainfall, the combined sewer system exceeds capacity. Untreated stormwater mixed with sewage discharges directly into the Gowanus Canal through CSO outfalls.

Backflow

High tides and storm surges push contaminated canal water back inland through drains and low-lying streets, carrying pollutants into the urban fabric.

Section 03

Case Studies

Three intervention strategies — compared through simulation, data, and trade-offs.

Strategy 01

Physical Block

A hard-engineered barrier system, floodwalls, gates, and bulkheads, designed to prevent canal water from entering streets.

Strengths

Immediate stop flood cessation
Predictable protection

Weaknesses

High cost
Ecological disruption
Obstructed tourist views

Water levels drop ~62% behind the barrier during a 100-year flood scenario, but minor inland accumulation persists due to rainfall trapped behind the wall.

Strategy 02

Green Infrastructure

Rain gardens, permeable pavements, and restored wetlands that slow down flood spreading, absorb and filter polluted sediment.

Strengths

Ecological co-benefits
Lower cost
Maintain Public space

Weaknesses

Limited capacity in extreme events
Requires longtime maintenance

Distributed green systems absorb up to 40% of small-storm runoff, significantly reducing CSO triggering frequency.

Peak runoff delayed by ~25 minutes — buying critical time for sewer system processing.

Strategy 03

Comprehensive Solution

A hybrid approach combining physical defenses, distributed green infrastructure, and policy-driven land use changes for layered resilience.

Strengths

Adaptive
Multi-scale
Balances ecology and protection

Weaknesses

Requires coordination across agencies
Requires long-term funding

Combined approach reduces flood depth by 78% across modeled scenarios — outperforming single-strategy interventions.

Pollutant load entering the canal drops by over half, with measurable ecological recovery within 5 years.
